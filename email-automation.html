Email Automation

Route incoming email, prepare context-aware drafts, schedule appropriate follow-up, and keep the right conversations visible to a person.

Audit your email workflow
Common opportunities

Reduce repeated handling while preserving context

Email automation works best when each action is connected to a clear business rule, customer expectation, and owner.

Inbox triage

Identify the topic and send the message to an appropriate queue without deleting the original.

Reply preparation

Build a draft from approved information, previous context, and the actual customer question.

Follow-up timing

Create a task or reminder when a response or internal action remains outstanding.

System updates

Capture useful details in a CRM, spreadsheet, or project record after validation.

Example inbox flow

Let automation prepare the next step, not hide the conversation

A reliable design preserves the source email, links actions to the correct thread, and makes it obvious when AI generated or changed content.

  • Match messages to an existing customer or project where appropriate
  • Detect missing attachments or required details
  • Route high-risk categories directly to people
  • Require review for commitments and sensitive replies
  • Log sending, failure, and opt-out events

Use cases

Different messages need different controls

One mailbox may contain routine questions, new leads, invoices, complaints, account changes, and private documents. They should not share one automation rule.

Customer requests

Prepare a response from current service information while escalating exceptions and commitments.

Internal approvals

Extract the request, notify the correct reviewer, and keep the decision attached to the original context.

Planned sequences

Send agreed onboarding, reminder, or follow-up messages with opt-out handling and stop conditions.

Draft and reviewContext stays visible before anything sends.
Email safeguards

Avoid automation that creates more cleanup

Before launch, define who owns bounced messages, conflicting records, unsupported requests, failed sends, replies to automated sequences, and customer corrections.

Can AI read every mailbox?

Access should be limited to the mailboxes, folders, and information required for the approved workflow.

Should drafts send automatically?

Only low-risk, well-tested categories should be considered for automatic sending. Many workflows should remain draft-and-review.

How do we avoid duplicate follow-up?

Use one system of record, idempotent steps, thread checks, and explicit stop conditions when a person responds.

Start with one mailbox

Map the messages your team repeatedly reads, routes, and rewrites

An audit can identify which categories are predictable enough to assist and which need a fully human response.
