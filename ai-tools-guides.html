Workflow selection checklist

Write the workflow as a simple sequence: trigger, inputs, decisions, actions, result, exceptions, and owner. Observe how the work really happens before redesigning it.

  • How often does it happen, and how much active effort does it consume?
  • What delay or inconsistency does it create for customers or staff?
  • Can the decision rules be explained and tested?
  • Are the required systems and data accessible with appropriate permission?
  • Can an error be detected, corrected, and learned from?

Tool selection checklist

Prefer a smaller stack that connects reliably to existing tools over a collection of impressive but isolated features.

  • Does it integrate through a supported connector, API, or reliable import/export?
  • Can access be limited by role, data type, and action?
  • Where is data processed and retained, and can it be deleted or exported?
  • What are the usage limits, ongoing costs, failure behavior, and support options?
  • Who can maintain the workflow when tools or business rules change?

Knowledge readiness checklist

AI should not be expected to resolve contradictory policies or guess which old file is still valid.

  • Name the authoritative source for each topic.
  • Remove duplicates and document conflicts.
  • Add ownership and a review date.
  • Keep source references visible in important responses.
  • Create a path for reporting missing or outdated information.

Human-control checklist

Set a lower automation boundary when the action carries greater risk or uncertainty.

  • Define which steps may draft, recommend, update, send, or approve.
  • Require review for sensitive, unusual, high-value, or low-confidence cases.
  • Keep a visible history of inputs, decisions, edits, and outputs.
  • Provide a manual fallback and a way to pause the workflow.
  • Tell users when they are interacting with automated support where appropriate.

Pilot testing checklist

Success on a few clean examples is not enough. Use representative inputs and deliberately difficult cases.

  • Test missing, malformed, duplicated, and conflicting information.
  • Check access with different roles and permissions.
  • Verify messages, records, and notifications reach the correct destination.
  • Observe how the workflow recovers from a disconnected tool or failed action.
  • Record false positives, missed cases, corrections, and user feedback.

Measurement checklist

Choose measures connected to the workflow itself. Avoid claiming broad business impact without a defensible link.

  • Time to first useful response
  • Age and size of the work queue
  • Completion of required handoffs
  • Percentage requiring correction or manual rescue
  • Staff effort for the same volume and quality of work
